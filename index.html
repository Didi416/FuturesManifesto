Introduction: Standing at the Crossroads

We live in an age where the systems we rely on to understand the world, such as our news feeds, search engines, and recommendation platforms, are no longer curated by editors or journalists alone. They are shaped by algorithms, programmed to optimise for metrics that rarely align with our wellbeing, but more to keep us doom-scrolling and addicted. These algorithms don't simply present reality; they filter it, prioritise it, and often distort it.

The paradox at the heart of our current system is simple yet profound: the stated goal of news platforms is to inform, but the actual optimisation target is engagement. Instead of helping us flourish as informed citizens, these systems are finely tuned to capture attention, maximise clicks, and extend time-on-site. Outrage and fear spread fast, and so that is what the algorithm gives us. The result is a feedback loop that polarises communities, degrades trust, and corrodes mental health.

The future I want to see flourish breaks this paradox. It is a future where information systems are designed not as attention traps but as infrastructures for psychological wellbeing, cultural preservation, and community cohesion.

The "thorny question" I pose in this manifesto is: What if the very systems we depend on to provide global knowledge are built to misinform and divide? And what would it take to remake them into systems that nurture rather than exploit us?

2

The Thorny Question: The Algorithmic Paradox

When I began my research through horizon scanning, one theme emerged repeatedly: the paradox of the algorithm. At their core, algorithms are neutral tools, optimising whatever target we give them. The problem is that our current targets are fundamentally misaligned with human flourishing.

Platforms say they want to "inform" or "connect" people, but what they actually measure are metrics and analytics like time spent scrolling, ad impressions, and click-through rates. These metrics are not measures of truth, trust, or wellbeing. They are measures of attention extraction. And because what captures attention most effectively are extreme emotions – fear, anger, novelty – the algorithm learns to feed us more of it.

This is the paradox: a tool designed to serve knowledge ends up serving division.

The thorny question then becomes: can algorithms ever truly optimise for something as complex as human flourishing? Or are we forever trapped in business models that treat us not as citizens with agency but as data points to be mined?

3

Futures Method: Horizon Scanning as Lens

To approach this question, I used horizon scanning, a futures-thinking method that identifies weak signals (low-visibility developments with high-impact potential). This method allowed me to see beyond the noise of the present and glimpse where small but significant shifts might lead.

What I found is that many of the technologies that could reshape news ecosystems in the future are emerging in entirely different domains:

  • Gaming addiction research could be adapted for news feeds to produce models for identifying harmful consumption patterns.
  • Biometric monitoring systems in cars — tracking eye movement, heart rate, and stress — offer frameworks for emotion-aware content delivery.
  • Accessibility technologies, originally designed to support disabled users, reveal new pathways for preserving culture across displaced communities.

These intersections were crucial. They showed me that futures often emerge not from entirely new inventions, but from interrelationships between existing systems. Futures thinking isn't just prediction — it's pattern recognition, seeing how developments in one domain ripple into another.

Scenario: 2050 and the Era of Climate Migration

By 2050, more than 20 island nations and territories will face the existential threat of climate displacement. Rising seas, stronger storms, and eroded ecosystems will push entire populations to migrate, with Australia emerging as a primary destination for Pacific climate refugees.

Migration on this scale is not just about logistics of shelter or food—it is about cultural survival. Communities forced to leave their homelands risk losing languages, traditions, knowledges, and histories. Preserving culture becomes a form of resilience, a way for displaced people to retain identity and continuity in the face of upheaval.

In this scenario, information systems become cultural infrastructures. Platforms like SBS, originally broadcasters of multicultural stories, could transform into cultural preservation platforms. Imagine "Culture Capsules" as digital archives where displaced communities store language recordings, oral histories, songs, recipes, and rituals; rich cultures preserved across generations and communities.

But this cultural infrastructure must also extend beyond archiving. Algorithms that today amplify division must instead be re-engineered to build bridges: fostering mutual understanding between host and migrant communities, countering xenophobia, and amplifying marginalised voices rather than silencing them.

This is not just a nice-to-have — it is a survival requirement for the societies of this century. Without such infrastructures, migration risks fuelling conflict, not cohesion.

Principles for Algorithmic Futures

Drawing on my horizon scan, I propose seven principles that should guide the design of next-generation news systems. Together, they form a manifesto for algorithmic futures that nurture rather than exploit.

1. Biometric-Aware Optimisation
Instead of optimising for engagement, systems can monitor user wellbeing, tracking stress, fatigue, or emotional overload, with consent and transparency. Algorithms could then prioritise content that supports calm, balance, and resilience.

2. Transparency Standards
Just as newspapers explain their editorial decisions, algorithms should be explainable. Users should be able to see not just what content they are shown, but why it was chosen, and what data shaped the decision.

3. Cultural Preservation Infrastructure
Content from displaced and marginalised communities must not be buried by unfortunate circumstances. Algorithms should be used to actively elevate minority voices as part of preserving culture in the era of mass migration.

4. User-Controlled Interventions
Instead of paternalistic censorship, systems should offer opt-in interventions against harmful patterns like doom-scrolling or filter bubbles. Users could set their own thresholds for when and how interventions occur.

5. Multi-Modal Information Design
Audio-first platforms are rising, but audio cannot replace the depth of text. Future systems should integrate formats — audio for accessibility, text for critical analysis, video for immersion — so that information is both convenient and deep.

6. Community-Led Content Creation
Algorithms cannot substitute for real community voices. Platforms must hire, support, and amplify content created by the people they aim to serve, ensuring representation is authentic, not tokenistic.

7. Migration-Era Design Standards
Information systems must be built with displacement in mind - designed to foster cultural bridges between communities, counter misinformation, and cultivate cohesion.

These principles are not just technical guidelines. They shift the role of algorithms from extractive tools to social infrastructures of resilience.

6

Professional and Societal Implications

For me personally, this manifesto reflects the professional path I want to take: to work at the intersection of technology, ethics, and culture. I do not want to design systems that strip-mine human attention or feed on division. I strive to contribute to infrastructures that support cultural survival, wellbeing, and resilience in the face of global crises like climate migration.

The stakes could not be higher. Our information systems are not peripheral – they shape our sense of reality itself. If they continue to optimise for outrage, we risk deepening polarisation at precisely the moment when humanity needs unity and cohesion most. If they can be redirected towards wellbeing and cultural preservation, they can become lifelines for displaced communities and foundations for social cohesion.

This isn't science fiction. The technologies exist today. What is missing is not capability but the strength of will and ethical clarity.

7

Reflexive Insight: Futures as a Choice

What this subject has taught me most is that the future is not fixed. Through horizon scanning, scenario building, and speculative prototyping, I've learned to see the future not as something inevitable but as a set of branching possibilities shaped by choices we make now and weak signals we can cultivate.

The algorithmic paradox feels overwhelming because the present trajectory is so entrenched. But futures thinking shows that even entrenched systems can be reimagined. The question is not whether change is possible, but rather it is whether we are willing to prioritise human flourishing over profit.

8

Closing Statement

We stand at a crossroads illuminated by the glow of our screens. Down one path lies the continuation of the status quo: algorithms that fragment our societies, exhaust our minds, and reduce our cultures to commodities. Down the other lies a possibility: systems designed to honour human complexity, preserve endangered cultures, and help us live together in unity.

This manifesto is both a warning and an invitation. A warning that the paradox will not resolve itself. And an invitation to consciously create the futures we want, rather than sleepwalking into the ones we fear.

The technology is already here. The decision is ours.

Which future will we build?
